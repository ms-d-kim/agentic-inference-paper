## 1. Framing

**Partition principle.** Segments are cut by **primary economic role with respect to the agentic-inference (serving) layer** — *what defines your business in relation to the GPUs that generate tokens for agents* — not by what activity you happen to perform. This is the only axis that stays MECE once you notice that the same company (OpenAI, Google) trains models *and* runs GPUs *and* ships agents. We resolve those collisions with explicit precedence rules (§5), assigning each actor to the **single bucket that defines it**.

**How to read this (3 lines).**
1. Eight of these are **actors** (humans/orgs with budgets and contracts); one — "the agent runtime as a machine client" — is **not an actor but the workload itself**, the request pattern every actor emits, kept in the map because it *is* the serving spec a PM designs against.
2. For each segment, the load-bearing columns are **inference touchpoint** (direct buyer vs. indirect demand-shaper, and *what they actually buy*) and **PM priority** — read those first if you only have two minutes per segment.
3. NVIDIA sells serving software/GPUs to **at most three of these segments directly**; the rest are demand-shapers, SLO-setters, or standards levers — they decide whether the NVIDIA stack *looks* efficient and legible to the segments that do pay.

**The non-obvious point.** The literal *user* of an inference service is increasingly **not a person or a company — it is an agent**, a machine client that issues a long, correlated stream of stateful calls (growing prefix, tool-gap pauses, re-entry, fan-out, retries) across one task. The humans and orgs "using agents" sit *behind* that client and never touch the serving contract directly. An NVIDIA inference PM's product is shaped by the **machine client's** request signature (KV reuse, resume, priority, hints), while the **purchase order** is signed by a different segment entirely (operators, labs, self-hosting enterprises). Conflating "who uses the agent" with "who uses the inference service" is the single most common segmentation error this map exists to prevent.

## 3. Per-Segment Deep Sections

### Segment 1 — Agent end-users (human consumers)

> **Read this segment as a workload spec and SLO-setter, not a customer. NVIDIA never sells to these humans; their volume and intolerance for tail latency *define the serving contract* the real buyers must satisfy.**

**Who + sub-segments.** The humans who consume agent *output* to get their own tasks done, with no relationship to the serving layer beyond "I press go and wait."
- **Prosumers / power individuals** — ChatGPT Plus ($20)/Pro ($200), Gemini Advanced, Claude, Perplexity Comet, plus "message-it-like-a-friend" agents over WhatsApp/Telegram/Signal (OpenClaw/Manus). Heaviest per-capita token burners; first to hit the new metered caps.
- **Enterprise knowledge workers (assistant-in-the-flow-of-work)** — hundreds of millions of seats in Microsoft 365 Copilot (Wave 3 / Cowork / Work IQ), Gemini Enterprise, Claude, embedded in Outlook/Word/Excel/Docs/Slack. IT picks the model and stack; the worker is a seat-licensed *indirect* consumer.
- **Non-technical line-of-business users** — sales, marketing, legal ops, finance ops, HR consuming vertical/no-code agents through a chat box or sidebar; zero tolerance for half-finished automations they cannot debug.

**Real players.** ChatGPT Plus/Pro Agent Mode + Deep Research; Google Gemini app + Gemini Spark (24/7 background agent); Microsoft 365 Copilot (KPMG global rollout via Agent 365); Claude app + Claude Cowork; Perplexity Comet; Manus (acquired by Meta ~Dec 2025), Genspark; OpenClaw; Gemini Enterprise.

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Bounded, predictable cost after the "Tokenpocalypse"** — know a task won't blow a monthly cap or trigger a surprise metered bill (agent runs silently burn many× a chat turn the user can't see).
2. **Fast, steady TTFT + visible progress** — consistent latency beats occasionally-fast/often-slow, especially on 5–30-min missions where a human stares at a spinner.
3. **Task actually completes and is trustworthy** — a finished, correct deliverable matters more than raw speed; non-technical users can't recover from a hallucinated automation.
4. **Peak-hour availability without throttling** — diurnal, bursty traffic must not degrade at 10am Monday.
5. **Continuity/memory across sessions and days** — Auto-Memory and scheduled overnight runs only feel magical if context persists and re-entry is cheap.
6. **Privacy/data residency they can take on faith** — in-tenant assurance without understanding the serving topology.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **Surprise caps & metered bills killed the "just use it" feeling.** *Root cause:* agent loops re-send the full, growing conversation history every turn (roughly quadratic token growth), so flat-rate access was extracting large effective subsidies; vendors metered it. The cost lives in prefill of long, redundant context the user never sees.
- **Long tasks feel slow and unaccountable.** *Root cause:* multi-step loops serialize LLM calls with tool-use pauses; with no warm KV reuse across steps, re-entry TTFT is high and the product rarely surfaces progress.
- **Inconsistent latency / peak-hour degradation erodes trust faster than absolute speed.** *Root cause:* sharing GPU capacity with chat and other tenants, queueing during diurnal peaks blows up p95/p99 tail latency; humans feel variance more than the mean.
- **Half-finished or wrong autonomous results they can't diagnose.** *Root cause:* a tool-call timeout, context overflow, or truncated decode surfaces as a confidently-wrong deliverable with no actionable error.
- **Account/model sprawl.** *Root cause:* the assistant market fractured into specialists, each with its own caps, latency profile, and memory silo, so the user re-pays and re-explains context across stacks.
- **Quiet quality/availability regressions when the vendor optimizes cost on the back end.** *Root cause:* users sit at the far end of operator decisions (quantization, batching, cheaper-model routing) and can't tell a model swap from a bad day.

**Workload signature.** Barbell: short interactive copilot turns in the body (summarize/draft/answer) with a fast-growing tail of long-horizon autonomous missions and a rising always-on/scheduled band (Gemini Spark, overnight Claude jobs). Tenancy is **almost entirely shared-with-chat, multi-tenant, interactive** — these users never get dedicated capacity. To a serving engine: highly variable idle-then-spike arrival, heavy prefill of long+redundant context on every agent re-entry, frequent tool-gap pauses stranding KV, and extreme p95/p99 TTFT sensitivity because a human is watching.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** Fully **indirect / non-buyers**. They buy a $20/$200 subscription or get a seat from IT; NVIDIA revenue is laundered through the operators and labs serving the traffic. Their importance is as the **demand signal and SLO-setter** (~1.2B AI-app WAU, bursty diurnal shape, tail-latency intolerance) — never a line on a purchase order.

**Buying behavior.** Decider: the individual for their own sub; IT/procurement for enterprise seats; the department head or a citizen-developer for LoB vertical agents. Prosumers proven to pay $20 and a cohort $200/mo but newly cap-aware and churn-prone after 2026; enterprises pay $30+/seat but now scrutinize realized usage and tolerate multi-assistant sprawl. **Buying trigger:** a recurring task the agent visibly finishes faster than by hand. **Anti-trigger (churn):** hitting a cap, a surprise bill, or a string of half-finished/slow runs.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** Treat this segment as the **workload spec, not a customer**, and build the serving features that let operators (NVIDIA's real buyers) keep these humans happy *while the price meter is now on*. Sharpest wedge: make long-context agent re-entry cheap and tail latency tight under bursty multi-tenant load — aggressive cross-step/cross-session KV reuse and prefix caching in Dynamo so the quadratic re-prefill behind the "Tokenpocalypse" isn't paid on every step, plus tenancy-aware scheduling that protects interactive p95/p99 TTFT for human-watched sessions sharing GPUs with chat. *Differentiation note:* Dynamo reports **85–97% cache hit on Claude-Code traffic — but that is a realized, single-session figure under Dynamo's own KV-aware routing, not an infinite-cache ceiling and not a multi-tenant result.** The multi-tenant, shared-with-chat case these users represent is the open gap.

**GRIT relevance.** This segment is the demand-side justification for cost-per-verified-**task** — the unit flat-rate's collapse exposed (what a *completed* Deep-Research run costs, not what a token costs). Their two top pains are exactly the realized-vs-available KV-locality gap: the redundant re-prefill on every re-entry is the locality left on the table, and closing it is what makes a metered task affordable *and* lowers re-entry TTFT. Their defining feature — shared-with-chat tenancy — is the exact condition GRIT proposes to characterize.

**Trajectory.** Explosive but shifting: total AI-app WAU ~20×'d in two years to ~1.2B (Feb 2026). Three concurrent shifts reshape the load: (1) autonomy moving from copilot to always-on/background (Gemini Spark); (2) the surface moving onto messaging and into the flow of work; (3) the April–June 2026 "Tokenpocalypse" permanently ending flat-rate, turning "cost per finished task" into the engagement-defining metric — the pressure that flows straight down to NVIDIA's serving stack.

### Segment 2 — Agent product builders (AI-native / commercial)

**Who + sub-segments.** VC-backed, AI-native companies whose shipped product *is* an agent — it autonomously plans, calls tools, and acts over multiple turns — built on foundation models + a serving layer and monetized directly.
- **Coding agents (IDE copilots + autonomous SWE)** — Cursor/Anysphere, Windsurf, Cognition/Devin, Replit Agent. Highest token intensity; bimodal (low-latency inline completion vs. long-horizon repo edits). Pioneered metered/ACU pricing.
- **Web / computer-use agents** — Genspark, Manus, Perplexity Comet, Browserbase/Browser-use. Multimodal: each step is screenshot+DOM→action; vision tokens dominate; sessions long and bursty.
- **Research / deep-research agents** — Perplexity deep research, Genspark research, You.com. Fan-out into parallel retrieval+read+synthesize sub-agents, then a long final-synthesis call.
- **Vertical-SaaS agents (regulated)** — Harvey (legal), Abridge (health), Sierra/Decagon (support). Lower autonomy but eval-pass gates, long documents, and compliance inflate cost; often need dedicated/private deployments.

**Real players.** Cursor/Anysphere (~$2B ARR Feb 2026; SpaceX $60B acquisition option Apr 2026); Cognition/Devin ($1B raise at $26B; metered Agent Compute Units; Goldman/Citi/NASA); Windsurf; Replit Agent; Genspark/MainFunc (Series B expanded to $385M Apr 2026); Manus; Perplexity (Comet, iOS Mar 2026); Sierra (>$150M ARR Jan 2026); Decagon (~$4.5B val); Harvey (~$11B val, ~$195M ARR); Abridge (~$5.3B val); Reflection AI.

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Predictable, low cost-per-completed-task (not per-token)** — gross margin is set by how many model calls a task takes; they must forecast $/task to price seats vs. metered/outcome tiers.
2. **High KV-cache / prefix reuse across the loop** — the single biggest lever on both latency and bill; the bulk of every turn's prompt is a repeated system prompt + tools + growing history.
3. **Low, stable TTFT/inter-token latency for interactive modes** (inline completion, chat-in-IDE, voice support) — coexisting with their own batch/long-horizon traffic.
4. **Reliable long-context handling as horizon grows** — context inflates turn-over-turn without blowing latency, cost, or accuracy.
5. **Burst/elastic capacity for fan-out** without paying for peak 24/7.
6. **Per-task / per-tenant cost and cache attribution** — to find unprofitable workflows and defend margins to the board.
7. **Model/provider portability** — route by price-performance, fine-tune open models, avoid a single lab raising prices or competing with them.
8. **For regulated verticals:** private/dedicated/region-pinned serving with eval gates baked in.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **The surprise bill: cost-per-completed-task explodes super-linearly with horizon even as $/token is flat or falling.** *Root cause:* each turn re-sends the entire growing context; compute is roughly quadratic in context and the loop runs many turns. No tool reports $/task, so it surfaces only as a multiple-× bill at scale.
- **Prompt/KV caching silently stops paying off on exactly their long-horizon traffic.** *Root cause:* agent context mutates every turn (tool outputs, retrieved docs, traces), invalidating the cached prefix; the advertised cache discount evaporates and they pay full prefill on every step. (cf. *Don't Break the Cache*, arXiv 2601.06007 — a provider-API-level quantification, not open-infra internals.)
- **The token tax caps gross margin well below SaaS, breaking flat-seat pricing.** *Root cause:* inference is COGS that scales with usage; heavy users on flat seats are loss-making, forcing the segment into metered/ACU/outcome pricing they must reverse-engineer from per-task cost they can't measure. *(The often-cited "~30 points below SaaS / 6–23% of revenue" figures are illustrative market talking points, not measured constants — present as range-with-source, not a precise anchor.)*
- **Interactive latency degrades when their own bursty long-horizon traffic shares serving capacity.** *Root cause:* long autonomous jobs and research fan-out hog prefill/decode slots and evict caches on the shared pool — a noisy-neighbor effect against themselves.
- **Dependence on a model provider that is also a competitor and price-setter.** *Root cause:* they run on OpenAI/Anthropic/Google models, but those labs now ship rival agents (Claude Code, Codex, Operator, Gemini deep research) and can change price, rate-limits, or deprecate models.
- **Build-vs-buy serving is a painful, blind decision.** *Root cause:* self-hosting only breaks even above a sustained-volume threshold and adds ML-systems headcount; builders lack a clean $/verified-task comparison across API vs. dedicated vs. self-host.
- **Orchestration overhead is invisible COGS.** *Root cause:* retries, internal planning steps, embeddings, vector queries, and eval passes add substantial overhead billed at the same rate as user-facing tokens, understating true cost-per-task until the bill arrives.

**Workload signature.** Heterogeneous and multimodal (text + vision). Bimodal autonomy: (a) interactive copilot/inline/voice = short context, tight TTFT; (b) long-horizon autonomous = a single "task" is tens-to-hundreds of model calls with monotonically growing context, frequent tool-gap pauses, re-entry, fan-out into parallel sub-agents (research), and heavy retry/eval loops. To a serving engine: long, repeatedly-re-submitted prefixes that *should* hit a prefix/KV cache but mutate each turn; bursty arrival; huge-prefill/small-decode (synthesis) and small-prefill/streaming-decode (completion) interleaved. Tenancy mostly shared-with-chat on hosted API today, shifting toward dedicated/reserved and partial self-hosting as volume and margin pressure rise, and toward region-pinned private serving for regulated verticals.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** **Indirect today, becoming direct/hybrid at scale.** Default path: buy tokens from a lab (OpenAI/Anthropic/Google) or a hosted open-model operator (Together/Fireworks/Baseten/Modal) — NVIDIA revenue captured one layer down. As volume crosses the self-host break-even and margin bites, they move to (1) dedicated/reserved deployments on an operator (still indirect, NVIDIA-stack underneath); (2) self-hosting fine-tuned open models (Llama/Qwen/GLM/Kimi/DeepSeek) on vLLM/SGLang on reserved neocloud (CoreWeave/Lambda/Nebius) or DGX Cloud — becoming **direct** consumers of TensorRT-LLM / NIM / Dynamo and candidates for NVIDIA Inception. What they'd buy: **NIM** (drop-in optimized endpoints for open models + fine-tunes), **TensorRT-LLM** (squeeze $/task on owned GPUs), **Dynamo** (disaggregated + KV-aware routing to recover lost reuse), **DGX Cloud** for burst/dedicated tenancy. They essentially never rack bare-metal GPUs themselves.

**Buying behavior.** Decider: founder/CTO or VP Eng / head of infra; for build-vs-buy, an ML-systems/inference lead. Inference is now a board-level COGS line scrutinized by the CFO. WTP is high but ROI-gated and ruthlessly $/task-anchored; they switch providers/models fast (low API-layer switching cost). **Triggers:** the surprise bill / a margin-threatening usage spike, a funding round forcing a path-to-margin story, a latency regression hurting retention, or a model-provider price/rate-limit/competitive shock. They prototype on serverless APIs, then graduate to reserved/dedicated/self-host. Procurement is fast, technical, benchmark-driven — they trust their own evals over vendor decks.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** Ship a **KV-reuse-and-attribution wedge for mutating-context agent loops, metered in cost-per-verified-task** — Dynamo KV-aware routing + cross-turn/disaggregated KV reuse + NIM, with a per-task/per-tenant cost-and-cache-hit dashboard. *Why now:* their #1 and #2 pains (super-linear $/task and caching that dies within a few turns on exactly long-horizon traffic) are precisely the failure generic prefix-cache reuse does *not* solve, and inference is the COGS line capping their margins — so a serving stack that demonstrably recovers reuse on growing/mutating context and proves it in $/task is what pulls indirect builders into direct NVIDIA deployments at the scale moment they're forced off naive APIs. Land it via the operators they already rent from plus DGX Cloud/Inception for self-hosters. *Magnitude honesty:* the "caching collapses within 3–5 turns" and "5–12× savings" figures are reported in synthetic/managed studies (cf. Sutradhara 2601.12967, *synthetic at production scale*; Dynamo blog); the real-traffic multi-tenant magnitude is unquantified — that gap is what the measurement closes.

**GRIT relevance.** Cost-per-verified-task is the unit detached from per-token billing that this segment lives and dies by; the realized-vs-available KV-locality gap is the mechanism behind their margin pain. (Academic anchor: **Cost-of-Pass**, ICLR 2026, arXiv 2504.13359 — expected $ for a *correct* solution; GRIT moves it from the API black box into open-infra internals.)

**Trajectory.** Fastest-growing, most capital-intensive consumer-facing layer of the agent stack in 2026 (coding leads; Devin enterprise usage up >10× since Jan 2026). Two structural shifts: (1) pricing migrates from flat seats to metered/ACU/outcome as the token tax makes flat seats loss-making — forcing $/task accounting into the open; (2) serving migrates from serverless API toward fine-tuned open models on dedicated/self-hosted GPUs as volume crosses break-even — pulling this segment from indirect toward direct NVIDIA consumption. Existential overhang: labs shipping competing agents and setting the price of the model layer.

### Segment 3 — Enterprise internal agent teams

**Who + sub-segments.** Platform/AI teams inside *non-AI-native* organizations that build and operate agents for their own employees or customers, where the agent is a cost-saving capability layered onto an existing business — not the product they sell.
- **Regulated, sovereignty-bound builders (banks, healthcare, gov, defense)** — cannot route customer/PHI data to public APIs (GDPR Art.46, DORA, HIPAA, NIS2, export control); forced into VPC/on-prem/air-gapped self-hosting — the strongest direct-GPU-buying motion.
- **Employee-productivity copilot teams (cross-industry)** — internal RAG copilots for tens-to-hundreds of thousands of staff; bursty, business-hours-shaped, shared-with-chat; start on API, migrate to self-host as volume crosses the break-even.
- **Customer-facing care/ops automation teams (telco, retail, banking)** — their own support/care agents and back-office ops (claims, fraud triage, network ops); long-horizon, tool-heavy, SLA-bound, 24/7 — most likely to justify dedicated warm capacity.
- **Platform / center-of-excellence teams** — build the internal "AI factory" platform (Wells Fargo Tachyon pattern, model-agnostic) other LoB teams build on. The actual NVIDIA AI Enterprise / Dynamo / NIM buyer inside the enterprise; KPI is GPU $/task across many tenants.

**Real players.** JPMorgan Chase (LLM Suite to ~250k employees; Payments Optimization Engine); Wells Fargo (Fargo on Gemini Flash + internal models; Tachyon platform); Citigroup (firm-wide copilots, ~175k employees enabled); Bank of America (Erica + internal copilots); SAP (Joule agents via Joule Studio on BTP using NVIDIA Agent Toolkit/NeMo); ServiceNow (Now Assist using Nemotron + Apriel); Siemens (Fuse EDA AI Agent on Nemotron); Novo Nordisk/Viva Biotech; AT&T/Verizon/Deutsche Telekom (DT's sovereign "industrial AI cloud" with NVIDIA); Walmart/Lowe's; EU sovereign-AI/national AI-factory programs.

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Data sovereignty + governed deployment as a first-class option** — in-VPC/on-prem/air-gapped with full audit trails (DORA, GDPR Art.46, HIPAA, NIS2, EU AI Act). The gating requirement, not a feature.
2. **A defensible cost-per-resolved-task number** to clear the ROI/POC-graveyard bar — CFOs demand $/successful-outcome, not tokens/sec.
3. **Turnkey, supported, model-agnostic serving a small platform team can run** — pre-optimized containers (NIM) + reference architectures to avoid the hidden engineering cost of a hand-rolled vLLM stack.
4. **Integration with systems of record + tool-calling reliability** — core banking, EHR, ERP, OSS/BSS, ServiceNow.
5. **Multi-tenant GPU efficiency under bursty business-hours traffic** — collapse idle reserved capacity into one efficient pool.
6. **Guardrails, PII redaction, decision auditability baked into the serving path** — to pass model-risk-management and regulator review.
7. **Model optionality / no lock-in** — route across open (Llama, Nemotron, Qwen) and frontier APIs so a deprecation doesn't strand a production agent.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **POCs die before production; the CFO can't see ROI.** *Root cause:* no accepted unit economic for agent work — vendors report tokens/sec, but an agent that retries, re-plans, and fails silently has a cost-per-*successful*-task nobody measures, so finance sees runaway token bills with no provable outcome and pulls funding. *(The widely-circulated "88–95% of pilots fail" figure is an analyst/marketing stat absent from this project's verified ledger — use the mechanism, not the number, as load-bearing.)*
- **Self-hosting math doesn't close because reserved GPUs sit largely idle.** *Root cause:* internal agent traffic is bursty and business-hours-shaped (and for copilots, shared-with-chat), so a reserved cluster is paid for 24/7 but used a few hours a day; per-task cost balloons and the build case loses to APIs. *(The specific "~5% utilization" figure is not in the verified ledger and is dominated by procurement/over-provisioning, not Dynamo's scheduler — present the dynamic, not the number.)*
- **Compliance/sovereignty forces self-hosting onto teams with no inference-serving competence.** *Root cause:* GDPR Art.46/DORA/HIPAA forbid public APIs, but the enterprise has bankers and clinicians, not kernel engineers; a hand-rolled OSS stack hides large ongoing DevOps/ML labor, so "free" open models become the most expensive option.
- **Long-horizon multi-step agents are far more expensive/slower than the pilot suggested.** *Root cause:* the agent re-sends a growing context every step and pauses at every tool call; on a naive stack the paused session's KV is evicted and recomputed on re-entry (prefill tax) — invisible in a single-shot demo, brutal at fleet scale.
- **Auditability and model-risk-management block go-live.** *Root cause:* regulators/MRM require a traceable, reproducible record of every agent decision and the data it touched; most stacks treat the LLM call as a black box with no built-in decision log or PII boundary.
- **Vendor/model lock-in risk on a 3–5-year regulated deployment.** *Root cause:* an agent calibrated to one model breaks when that model is deprecated or repriced; multi-year change-control can't absorb that, so they demand a model-/service-agnostic platform.

**Workload signature.** Dominated by enterprise support/care, internal knowledge copilots, and back-office ops automation — heavily RAG- and tool-grounded against systems of record, with multi-agent orchestration emerging in survivors. Production mass is copilot + supervised long-horizon (human-in-the-loop, audit-gated). **Mixed tenancy is the defining trait:** employee copilots are bursty, business-hours, often shared-with-chat; customer-care/ops agents are SLA-bound and pushing toward 24/7 dedicated warm capacity. To a serving engine: spiky multi-tenant prefill-heavy sessions with long, repeatedly-resent RAG/context prefixes, frequent tool-gap pauses and re-entry, and a long idle overnight tail — low average utilization punctuated by sharp daytime bursts, with strict in-perimeter and audit constraints on every request.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** **Direct for the self-hosting (regulated/sovereign) sub-segment; indirect for the rest.** When sovereignty forces in-perimeter serving, the platform/CoE team buys **NVIDIA AI Enterprise + NIM** (pre-optimized inference containers) — often on DGX systems or an on-prem/VPC "AI factory" reference design, **TensorRT-LLM** under the hood, **Dynamo** for multi-node KV-aware disaggregated serving once they outgrow single-node NIM, with **Nemotron** open weights increasingly the default (ServiceNow/SAP/Siemens patterns). The non-sovereign majority touches NVIDIA *indirectly* via DGX Cloud, a neocloud, a hyperscaler-hosted NIM, or through ISVs (Now Assist, Joule) that embed NVIDIA serving. Few build raw GPU + vLLM themselves — the whole NIM/AI-Enterprise pitch is "don't."

**Buying behavior.** Committee-driven and slow: CIO/CTO or Chief AI Officer sponsors, a platform/CoE team specs, and Risk/Compliance/Security/Procurement/MRM all hold veto. Budget: central IT/AI-platform opex (capex for on-prem GPU), increasingly carved from headcount-savings business cases. WTP is **high for compliance/sovereignty and support** ("insurance against fines") and for a credible ROI story, **low for raw performance bragging rights**. **Triggers:** a regulatory mandate, a board-level "where's our AI ROI," an API bill crossing the self-host break-even, or a flagship pilot that finally cleared MRM. Strong preference for supported, named-vendor, reference-architecture solutions; deeply lock-in-averse on 3–5-year horizons.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** Ship **NIM/Dynamo as the "governed agent factory in a box" for regulated self-hosters** — bundle KV-aware disaggregated serving with multi-tenant scheduling that absorbs the bursty business-hours profile (daytime copilot spikes and overnight ops batch share the *same* GPUs instead of each reserving idle capacity), plus per-request audit/PII-boundary hooks and a first-class cost-per-resolved-task dashboard. *Why now:* the #1 blocker is the ROI/utilization math and auditability — both serving-layer outcomes — not model quality. They have the mandate and budget but can't make reserved-but-idle GPUs pencil out or prove outcome ROI to a CFO who just killed a stack of pilots. Whoever turns "comply + prove ROI + don't hire kernel engineers" into a one-SKU answer wins the on-prem/VPC slice (analyst estimates put regulated-US on-prem/hybrid LLM workloads at ~$7–10B by 2027 — directional, not a verified number).

**GRIT relevance.** This segment is the ideal first customer because they *must* justify GPU spend to a hostile CFO and a skeptical regulator, and neither accepts tokens/sec. Cost-per-verified-task gives the CoE team the ROI number their business case needs — total cost across *all* attempts (including failed/retried) ÷ tasks that actually succeeded. The locality-tax fraction (the share of cost caused by KV-eviction and prefill recompute on tool-gap re-entry) is the lever that decides whether self-hosting pencils out; KV-locality measurement also directly attacks the low-utilization problem by showing whether shared-with-chat copilot tenancy is reusing prefixes or thrashing.

**Trajectory.** Inflecting hard in 2026 but bifurcated. New agentic use cases at the largest financial firms roughly doubled vs. late-2024; leading banks have put AI tools in front of large fractions of their workforce. But the dominant story is the **pilot-to-production wall** — most of the remaining work is data/governance/integration plumbing, not modeling — so spend is large and concentrating: survivors standardize on a few internal platforms and shift from "many cheap pilots on APIs" to "a few governed agents on owned/VPC GPUs." The regulated on-prem/hybrid slice is the part of this segment that most directly buys NVIDIA.

### Segment 4 — Agent framework & orchestration providers

**Who + sub-segments.** OSS-led software vendors whose product is the **control plane** that drives the agent loop — state, planning, tool-routing, multi-agent handoff, durable execution — while delegating every token-generating call to a model behind an (almost always OpenAI-compatible) client they do not host.
- **Graph/workflow control-plane runtimes** (LangGraph, Microsoft Agent Framework, LlamaIndex Workflows) — sell durable execution, checkpointing, re-entry. Their state model decides whether a paused agent's context is re-sent verbatim (cache-friendly) or rebuilt/trimmed (cache-busting) on resume — the single biggest lever on serving-side KV reuse.
- **Multi-agent / role-orchestration frameworks** (CrewAI, AutoGen-lineage, A2A delegation) — fan a task across many sub-agents sharing a large common prefix; each sub-agent opens a fresh stateless call today, so the prefix is recomputed N times.
- **Lab-native SDKs tied to a provider's serving stack** (OpenAI Agents SDK + Responses API, Strands + Bedrock) — push state server-side via `previous_response_id`/Conversations API, ceding KV locality to the lab; least likely to ever point at NVIDIA-served open weights.
- **Model-abstraction / routing layer inside frameworks** (LiteLLM, OpenAI-compatible providers in CrewAI/LangChain) — the thin client that turns "switch from GPT to Llama-on-vLLM" into a config change; the chokepoint deciding which endpoint receives traffic, and the layer that strips engine-specific cache/priority hints.

**Real players.** LangGraph/LangChain (Series B Oct 2025, ~$1.25B val; LangGraph Platform GA); CrewAI (v1.x, native MCP+A2A; ~12M daily agent executions reported); Microsoft Agent Framework 1.0 (GA Apr 3 2026, AutoGen + Semantic Kernel converged, both to maintenance mode); OpenAI Agents SDK; LlamaIndex (Workflows + LlamaCloud); Pydantic AI; AWS Strands; **NVIDIA NeMo Agent Toolkit** (ex-AIQ — framework-agnostic profiling + KV-cache optimization; NVIDIA's own entrant).

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Model/provider portability** — one code path targeting any OpenAI-compatible endpoint (frontier API today, self-hosted vLLM/NIM tomorrow) without rewrites.
2. **Durable, resumable state across tool-gap pauses and crashes** (LangGraph checkpointing, OpenAI Conversations API).
3. **Cheap, low-latency multi-turn/multi-agent calls** — because the framework re-sends a growing transcript every step, prefill cost compounds; they need the engine to make repeated prefixes nearly free.
4. **Standardized way to pass tools and context** (MCP for tool/context, A2A for cross-agent delegation) so the loop is portable.
5. **Per-step and per-task observability/attribution** (tokens, latency, $ at node/agent granularity) — today filled by LangSmith/NeMo-Toolkit profiler, not the engine.
6. **A neutral way to express intent to the serving layer** (latency-sensitive / prefix-reusable / speculative) without coupling to one engine's proprietary API.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **Their own abstraction destroys KV-cache reuse.** *Root cause:* the OpenAI-compatible Chat Completions contract they standardized on is stateless and prefix-exact-match only; in late turns most prefill cost is recompute of history, but the framework gives the engine no continuity signal.
- **Multi-agent fan-out recomputes a shared prefix N times.** *Root cause:* no cross-request/cross-agent KV-sharing primitive in the framework→engine path; each sub-agent looks like an unrelated tenant to the engine.
- **They cannot see or control per-task cost.** *Root cause:* the orchestration layer sits above the meter; cost-per-completed-task and cache hit/miss attribution live on the serving side, invisible across the OpenAI-compatible seam.
- **Provider-portability is shallow.** *Root cause:* caching/priority/state semantics are non-standard across endpoints; the LiteLLM-style lowest-common-denominator client only guarantees text-in/text-out, so engine-specific performance contracts don't survive.
- **Tool-gap pauses leave the engine guessing.** *Root cause:* no resume/affinity hint in the request contract; on re-entry the framework issues what looks like a brand-new request, so in-flight KV is evicted under load (the problem Continuum/CacheTTL-style scheduling targets — arXiv 2511.02230).
- **They're blamed for latency and spend they don't control.** *Root cause:* end-to-end latency is dominated by repeated prefill and queueing in the engine, but users experience it as the framework being slow; without engine-side hints the framework can only add retries and parallelism, often worsening contention.

**Workload signature.** The defining trait is the *shape*, not the domain: a long-horizon, multi-step loop issuing many sequential completions over a monotonically growing prefix (system prompt + tool schemas + accumulating transcript), punctuated by tool-gap pauses and re-entry, plus multi-agent fan-out where K sub-agents share a large common prefix. Autonomy ranges copilot → long-horizon → increasingly always-on/scheduled crews. Tenancy is the awkward part: the framework process is multi-tenant and bursty, but each request arrives at the engine as an independent, stateless, OpenAI-compatible call sharing a pool with chat — so without hints the engine sees high-prefix-overlap, high-recompute, pause-heavy traffic as unrelated noise.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** **Mostly indirect / non-buyer of GPUs, but the highest-leverage demand-shaper in the stack.** Frameworks own zero GPUs and rarely write NVIDIA a check; they *decide which endpoint the tokens land on*. NVIDIA touchpoints: (1) **NIM** as the OpenAI-compatible endpoint a framework points at for self-hosted open weights — frictionless drop-in is the adoption wedge; (2) **Dynamo** as the engine whose KV-aware routing/disaggregation/prefix cache the framework's traffic should light up — but only if the loop emits the right signals; (3) **NeMo Agent Toolkit**, NVIDIA's own entry, with framework-agnostic profiling, KV-cache optimization, and runtime hints bridging LangChain/CrewAI/Agno to Dynamo. NVIDIA monetizes this segment one hop downstream (via operators running NIM/Dynamo) while NeMo Agent Toolkit is its direct play to own the seam.

**Buying behavior.** Adoption is bottoms-up and developer-led: an engineer picks a framework by ergonomics, GitHub momentum, and docs — at $0 (MIT/Apache). The vendor monetizes the adjacent control/observability plane (LangGraph Platform, LangSmith, CrewAI Enterprise, LlamaCloud) sold to platform/eng leads, **not** the model spend. Crucially, *neither the developer nor the framework vendor pays NVIDIA* — the model/serving bill is a separate line the builder or enterprise owns. The trigger that matters to an inference PM is the **frontier-API-vs-self-host** decision the framework makes easy or hard: a framework shipping a first-class, cache-warm NIM path moves open-weight volume onto NVIDIA serving; one defaulting to a lab's server-side-state API keeps KV locality — and spend — inside the lab.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** **Ship a standard orchestrator-to-engine continuity contract and make NVIDIA the reference implementation.** Concretely: define a thin, framework-agnostic header/extension on the OpenAI-compatible call — `{session/affinity id, prefix-reusable, latency-sensitive, speculative-branch, hold-KV-during-tool-gap, priority}` — wire it into Dynamo's KV router and NIM, and land it upstream in LangGraph, CrewAI, and Microsoft Agent Framework via NeMo Agent Toolkit adapters. *Why now:* frameworks just standardized on MCP/A2A and stateless OpenAI-compatible calls, so their loops are leaking large recoverable prefill cost and the seam is still unowned; NeMo Agent Toolkit already does KV-cache optimization and latency-sensitivity inference. **Own the seam before it ossifies into a lab-proprietary stateful API** (Responses/Conversations) that routes KV locality — and revenue — away from NVIDIA-served open weights.

**GRIT relevance.** The framework is the one entity that can A/B the loop's *shape* but is blind to its serving cost. Measurement lets a framework prove: (1) that re-sending vs. resuming context, or sharing vs. recomputing a multi-agent prefix, changes cost-per-completed-task by the prefix-cache delta — turning a UX choice into a measured economic one; (2) which defaults (transcript trimming, fan-out granularity, retry policy) help vs. break KV locality on a real engine; (3) a vendor-neutral benchmark of cost-per-verified-task across endpoints (frontier API vs. NIM-on-Dynamo). For NVIDIA, this is the evidence layer that quantifies the value of the continuity hints NeMo Agent Toolkit is trying to optimize.

**Trajectory.** 2026 is consolidation + standardization: AutoGen and Semantic Kernel folded into Microsoft Agent Framework 1.0; MCP effectively won the tool/context layer and A2A is becoming the cross-agent delegation standard. The center of gravity is shifting from single-shot chains to durable, long-horizon, multi-agent crews, multiplying the re-send/fan-out recompute problem. Two forks diverge: lab-native SDKs pushing state server-side (to keep KV — and spend — captive) vs. open frameworks staying model-agnostic. NeMo Agent Toolkit is moving fast to be the optimization/hint layer under the open fork — the contested ground for the next 12–18 months.

### Segment 5 — Inference-serving operators (the core NVIDIA customer)

**Who + sub-segments.** Organizations that **own/operate the GPU fleet and serving stack** on which agent and LLM traffic actually executes — accountable for $/token, utilization, and latency SLOs. This is NVIDIA Dynamo/serving's **direct buyer**. *(Boundary, per §5: GPU custody is the test. A frontier lab serving its own weights is scored under Model providers, not here.)*
- **Inference-as-a-service / token vendors** (Together, Fireworks, Baseten, Modal, Anyscale, DeepInfra, Replicate, Fal) — sell tokens or per-minute GPU on shared multi-tenant fleets; compete on $/token and TTFT; most exposed to agentic economics because their customers (Cursor, Perplexity, Notion, Sourcegraph) drive bursty long-context traffic. Highest appetite for Dynamo-style disaggregation, KV reuse, and per-tenant attribution.
- **Neoclouds / GPU clouds** (CoreWeave, Lambda, Nebius, Nscale, Crusoe) — rent raw GPU (training heritage) now pivoting to sustained inference. Power, not GPUs, is the binding constraint, so tokens-per-watt and fleet utilization are existential; often resell the NVIDIA stack as a managed "Dedicated Inference" tier.
- **Hyperscaler managed-inference layers** (Azure, AWS, GCP) — largest fleets, abstract GPUs behind token APIs (Bedrock/Vertex/Foundry). Partly captive to their own silicon (Trainium/Inferentia, TPU) — a partner *and* a substitution threat.
- **Self-hosting enterprises (regulated/sovereignty-driven)** — run their own NVIDIA fleet for residency/compliance/cost-at-scale; lower utilization, single-tenant; buy NIM/Triton/TensorRT-LLM + AI Enterprise support precisely to avoid building disaggregation/KV plumbing.

**Real players.** Together AI; Fireworks AI ($4B val, $250M raise Oct 2025); Baseten ($6.50/hr H100 dedicated; VPC option); Anyscale; Modal (sub-1s cold starts); CoreWeave (250k GPUs, 43 DCs, >1GW active, $2.08B Q1'26 rev, NVIDIA Exemplar Cloud for GB200 NVL72, "Dedicated Inference"); Lambda (KC 10k Blackwell Ultra AI factory); Nebius (Azure capacity deal, 0.8–1GW by end-2026); Nscale/Stargate Norway; Azure/AWS/GCP managed inference; regulated self-hosting banks/telcos/defense/health on AI Enterprise + NIM.

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Maximize revenue-bearing utilization (goodput, not raw GPU%)** — keep GPUs serving SLO-passing tokens, not idle or doing wasted/preempted work. *(The "33–64% compute utilization" range cited in the wild conflates MFU, GPU%, and goodput and is not in the verified ledger — frame qualitatively: agentic mixed traffic leaves goodput on the table; the defensible quantity is realized-vs-available KV reuse under bounded cache + interleaving.)*
2. **High prefix/KV-cache hit rate on agentic traffic** — agents resend the full accumulated context every step (cf. Dynamo blog: 11.7× read/write on Claude-Code traffic), so reuse via RadixAttention / automatic prefix caching / Dynamo KV-manager is the single biggest per-call lever.
3. **Independent scaling of prefill vs. decode (disaggregation)** — separate compute-bound prefill from memory-bound decode (Dynamo/NIXL over RDMA) so each scales to its own bottleneck.
4. **Meet heterogeneous SLOs under bursty mixed traffic** — TTFT for interactive copilot turns *and* TTLT for long-horizon runs on the same fleet, without one starving the other.
5. **Per-tenant / per-agent cost and KV attribution** — to price, bill, set quotas, and protect margin (no system reaches per-request granularity today).
6. **Tokens-per-watt / power efficiency** — for neoclouds, power is the binding capacity constraint.
7. **Cold-start and capacity elasticity for spiky fan-out** — sub-second model load to absorb retry storms without overprovisioning.
8. **Turnkey production + enterprise support (self-hosters)** — one-command deploy, OpenAI-compatible endpoint, licensed support (NIM-style).

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **Agentic traffic is 5–30× more expensive per task than chat and margins erode, but operators can't explain or attribute the cost.** *Root cause:* agents resend full growing context and chain many calls; the engine is workflow-agnostic (blind to the loop), can't anticipate reuse, and exposes no per-request/per-tenant KV-and-GPU-second attribution.
- **KV-cache reuse collapses on agent loops because of tool-gap pauses.** *Root cause:* between turns the agent pauses to call a tool; default schedulers evict that session's KV, so on re-entry the prefix is recomputed from scratch. (Continuum/CacheTTL, arXiv 2511.02230, motivates TTL retention; the production multi-tenant magnitude is unquantified — that's the gap.)
- **Low effective goodput on mixed agent+chat fleets.** *Root cause:* prefill spikes interrupt decode and bursty fan-out plus uncertain decode length make batching inefficient.
- **Co-locating chat and long-horizon agents causes unpredictable SLO violations operators can't see why.** *Root cause:* a single long run can monopolize decode slots and KV memory, starving latency-sensitive copilot turns; without priority/tenancy controls and visibility, breaches surface after the fact.
- **The orchestrator↔engine seam is two black boxes.** *Root cause:* frameworks know the DAG, the coming tool pause, and shareable prefixes but don't tell the engine; the engine can't act on what it isn't told. A hint contract (Dynamo `agent_hints`, KVFlow) is emerging but unstandardized and its value is unmeasured on open infra.
- **Power, not GPU supply, is the growth ceiling (neoclouds).** *Root cause:* datacenter power procurement lags GPU availability; every watt on idle or recomputed-prefix work is forgone revenue capacity.
- **Operational complexity of disaggregated serving at scale.** *Root cause:* disaggregation needs a planner, smart router, distributed KV manager, and RDMA KV transfer (NIXL) working together — which is why smaller players buy NIM/Dynamo rather than build.
- **Substitution and circular-financing pressure on the NVIDIA relationship.** *Root cause:* hyperscalers push captive accelerators and neoclouds carry large NVIDIA-financed capex backlogs; if NVIDIA serving software doesn't deliver clear agentic advantages, the GPU's default position weakens.

**Workload signature.** To the engine this segment is the **aggregate of everyone else's traffic**, dominated by agentic patterns: heavy coding-agent traffic (high input:output ratios), web/computer-use, research, multi-agent fan-out, mixed with legacy chat on the same GPUs. A barbell of short interactive copilot turns (tight TTFT) and long-horizon autonomous runs (long, a-priori-uncertain decode; TTLT matters), plus always-on background agents. Structurally: long and growing context resent every step, tool-gap pauses then re-entry (the KV-eviction trap), bursty fan-out and retry storms, high cross-request prefix shareability. Tenancy: shared multi-tenant with chat for IaaS/neoclouds/hyperscalers (the source of SLO-interference and attribution pain); dedicated single-tenant but lower-utilization for self-hosters.

Hyperscalers are the largest GPU buyers but the biggest substitution risk. Self-hosting enterprises are the cleanest full-stack NIM/Triton/Dynamo + DGX Cloud buyers.

**Buying behavior.** Decider: VP Infra / Head of Inference / platform-eng leadership, with the platform/serving engineer (goodput, $/GPU-hr, cache-hit-rate owner) as technical evaluator and a CFO/FinOps capex gate. Budget: the infrastructure/COGS line — for IaaS and neoclouds, serving efficiency *is* gross margin. WTP high and tied to demonstrable margin or capacity unlock; sophisticated and benchmark-driven — they A/B before committing, and IaaS players build rather than buy if the OSS delta is small. **Triggers:** the agentic-traffic cost spike / margin compression, a customer SLO breach from chat+agent co-location, a capacity/power wall, the move from experimentation to sustained production, and competitive token-price pressure on Blackwell.

### Segment 6 — The agent runtime as a machine client ("agent-as-user")

> **This is not an actor and not a member of the actor partition — it is the WORKLOAD: the request pattern every serving-facing segment emits. It has no budget and signs no contract. It is profiled so a PM can design the serving primitives the budget-holding operator segment buys. (See §5 for why it is demoted off the actor partition.)**

**Who + sub-segments.** The stream of correlated, stateful calls an agent runtime issues to the serving layer across one task.
- **Long-horizon autonomous single-agent trajectory (coding/SWE)** — Claude-Code / Codex-CLI / Cursor-agent loop: one `program_id`, monotonically growing prefix (system prompt + tool defs + full action/observation history), tool-gap pauses (seconds) between turns. The canonical write-once-read-many stream.
- **Multi-agent fan-out / sub-agent teams** — a lead spawns N parallel specialists sharing an overlapping system prompt + tool defs; bursty concurrent prefill where cross-worker dedup and one-compute-N-load is the win.
- **Re-entrant / always-on / scheduled agents** — pause minutes-to-hours (human approval, cron, webhook, durable checkpoint) then re-enter; prefix has long since aged out of GPU/L1, demanding offload-to-CPU/disk tiering + session-affinity re-warm.
- **Conversational / copilot agent (shared-with-chat tenancy)** — each turn re-sends the conversation; competes for the same KV budget as interactive chat; the segment most exposed to stochastic stateless routing recomputing history every turn.
- **Speculative / retry-and-branch traffic** — idempotency-keyed retries and speculative tool prefill; duplicate/provisional prefills the engine must dedup by sequence hash and may discard.

**Real players (real-world embodiments, not buyers).** Claude Code / Codex CLI agent loops (cited by NVIDIA's Dynamo agentic blog at 11.7× read/write); NVIDIA Dynamo `nvext.agent_hints` + ThunderAgent program scheduler (arXiv 2602.13692); Anthropic Messages API `cache_control:{ephemeral, ttl}`; OpenAI Responses API `previous_response_id`; SGLang RadixAttention / vLLM Automatic Prefix Caching / TensorRT-LLM; llm-d + KV-aware routing; MCP tool calls (the source of the tool-gap pauses); research artifacts (KVFlow 2507.07400, KVCOMM 2510.12872, SparseX 2606.01751, TokenDance 2604.03143).

**Needs (ranked) — these *are* the serving contract.**
1. **KV-prefix reuse across turns within a trajectory** — the growing prefix must be served from cache, not recomputed; at high input:output ratios, recompute is the dominant cost and the reason complex agents are economically viable.
2. **Survive the tool-gap without losing the prefix** — a tool pause must not age the trajectory's KV out under LRU; the contract is lifecycle-aware retention (logical pause ≠ decode preemption; eviction deferred to tool boundaries).
3. **Session/trajectory affinity** — every turn of one `program_id` routed to the worker holding its blocks, so prefill latency doesn't scale linearly with depth via stochastic stateless balancing.
4. **A hint channel to declare its own shape** — priority, output-sequence-length, speculative_prefill, cache_control TTL, trajectory_final.
5. **Cross-worker / multi-tier dedup for fan-out** — N sub-agents sharing a prefix → one compute + (N-1) loads, eliminating the teammate cold-start gap.
6. **Re-entry / resume after long gaps** — offload-and-rehydrate so always-on and human-in-the-loop agents resume from a cache tier.
7. **Idempotent, dedup-safe retries and speculative branches** — keyed so the engine distinguishes a duplicate from a new call and discards speculative prefills cleanly.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **Tool-gap eviction.** *Root cause:* engines treat the GPU as stateless between requests and evict by recency; with no notion of agent lifecycle, idle-but-not-dead trajectories look identical to abandoned ones and lose blocks at the wrong moment.
- **Stochastic stateless routing recomputes history every turn.** *Root cause:* default balancers hash nothing about the session, so turn k+1 lands on a random worker that never held turn k's KV — locality that physically existed somewhere in the cluster is thrown away.
- **All KV blocks weighted equally despite radically different reuse value.** *Root cause:* LRU/uniform-cost eviction is blind to block semantics; without per-block value signals the cache cannot keep the WORM prefix hot and drop throwaway tokens. *(The claim that reasoning/scratch tokens are ~40% of output with zero post-loop reuse is a model-dependent design bet, not a measured serving invariant — soften to "reasoning/scratch tokens *may* have low post-turn reuse"; tagging them ephemeral is a hypothesis to measure, not a fact.)*
- **Fan-out cold-start.** *Root cause:* no cross-worker block sharing or sequence-hash dedup at admission, so a prefix on the lead's worker is recomputed on every teammate's worker. *(The specific "teammate ~79% vs. lead ~91% hit" split is workload-specific and not in the verified ledger — treat as a hypothesis to quantify.)*
- **Head-of-line blocking and unbounded GPU occupancy under load.** *Root cause:* request-level schedulers can't apply backpressure at the agent's natural pause points; there's no program-granularity admission control to demote ACTING programs and reclaim memory.
- **The trajectory cannot express its own needs.** *Root cause:* hint surfaces are vendor-specific and nascent (`nvext.agent_context`, `cache_control` TTL); most frameworks emit plain stateless completion calls.
- **Wasted spend on failed/abandoned trajectories.** *Root cause:* no per-trajectory cost attribution tied to task success; the serving layer bills tokens and can't see that a stream's GPU-seconds yielded nothing.

**Workload signature.** Predominantly coding/SWE and computer/web-use, long-input, reasoning-heavy; skewed to long-horizon autonomous + copilot (many sequential turns per task), with bursty multi-agent fan-out; *not* single-shot. To the engine: a single logical program emitting correlated requests sharing a monotonically growing prefix, high input:output ratio (cf. Dynamo blog 11.7× read/write — the one figure with a traceable source), punctuated by tool-gap pauses (GPU-idle but trajectory-alive), occasional minutes-to-hours re-entry, parallel fan-out with overlapping prefixes, and idempotent retries/speculative prefills. Tenancy: increasingly dedicated long-horizon trajectories but a large share still shared-with-chat — making it the workload whose **realized-vs-oracle cache-locality gap is largest and most policy-sensitive**.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** **Non-buyer (it is traffic) — but the demand signal that justifies the entire purchase.** Its real-world proxy at the API layer is the model-provider serving endpoint (Anthropic/OpenAI), where the trajectory exercises `cache_control`/`previous_response_id`. For NVIDIA the touchpoint is indirect-but-decisive: every agentic feature shipped (Dynamo KV-aware router, ThunderAgent program scheduler, `nvext.agent_context`, multi-tier KV offload, priority scheduling, snapshot fast-start) is justified by this signature. Treat this segment as the **spec**, the operator segment as the **customer**.

**Buying behavior.** No direct buyer — but it dictates what the operator and builder/enterprise segments pay for. **Buying trigger:** a step-change in cost-per-completed-task or tail TTFT when an org moves from single-shot chat to long-horizon agents and discovers recompute/eviction dominates the bill. WTP is high because reuse is multiplicative; the decision owner is the operator's infra/platform team choosing a serving stack, and framework vendors are influencers who decide which hints to emit. Lock-in accrues to whichever serving layer best honors the contract.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** **Make the agent trajectory a first-class scheduling object in Dynamo** — standardize and widen the agent-hints contract (`program_id`, `trajectory_final`, priority, osl, speculative_prefill, cache_control TTL) and the ThunderAgent program scheduler into the default path, then drive framework adoption so LangGraph/OpenAI-Agents-SDK/CrewAI emit these signals natively rather than firing stateless completions. The wedge *now*: **tool-boundary-aware retention + lifecycle-aware eviction** (logical pause, defer eviction to tool gaps, tag reasoning tokens ephemeral, resume smallest-token programs first) — the 2–30s tool-gap eviction pathology is universal across every agent product and no stateless engine handles it. Owning the trajectory abstraction (the contract *between* the framework segment and the operator segment) is how Dynamo becomes the default agentic serving layer before the protocol standardizes elsewhere (Anthropic `cache_control` / OpenAI Responses).

**GRIT relevance.** This segment *is* the unit of measurement. GRIT's identifying primitive — the same-stream oracle-vs-bounded Δ GPU-seconds per verified task (the locality gap) — is defined exactly over this trajectory: replay one agent stream against an infinite-cache oracle vs. the engine's realized hit counters to quantify how much physically-available locality the policy throws away, then divide wasted GPU-seconds by tasks the agent verified-completes (the "Cost of Grit," total cost over all attempts ÷ verified tasks). H2 (Cost of Grit grows super-linearly with horizon and is cache-policy-sensitive) is a statement about precisely this stream.

**Trajectory.** Steepest-growing workload class through 2026: agent/coding traffic is shifting the median request from short single-shot chat to long-horizon multi-turn trajectories, with multi-agent fan-out becoming common — driving disaggregation and KV-aware routing from research into production default, and the explicit motivation for Dynamo's ThunderAgent scheduler, `agent_context` hints, and multi-tier KV offload this cycle. As model providers expose server-side trajectory state (OpenAI Responses) and extended cache TTLs (Anthropic 1-h), the trajectory is becoming a named, addressable object — the request pattern is standardizing into a contract, which is exactly when a PM can win or lose the abstraction.

### Segment 7 — Adjacent tooling & ecosystem vendors

**Who + sub-segments.** Independent software vendors whose product **clamps onto** the agent+inference stack — none of which run the model or ship the agent, but which together define what the serving layer is asked to do and how its output is measured. *(Boundary, per §5: they hold no GPU custody — they observe, route, or meter across operators they don't own.)*
- **Observability & eval platforms** — trace/score/regression-test agent runs (LangSmith, Braintrust, Arize/Phoenix, Langfuse, Galileo, Laminar). Converging on OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions + OpenInference/OpenLLMetry. Consume serving telemetry; buy nothing from NVIDIA.
- **LLM gateways & routers** — multi-model routing, retries, semantic + exact caching, rate-limit/budget enforcement (Portkey, LiteLLM, OpenRouter, TrueFoundry, Kong AI Gateway, and the open llm-d / vLLM-semantic-router / Dynamo router). The sub-segment that physically touches KV locality.
- **Agent memory / RAG infra** — persistent cross-session state so context isn't re-stuffed each turn (Mem0, Zep/Graphiti, Letta/MemGPT, LangMem). *Directly competes with — and can substitute for — serving-side KV reuse.*
- **Guardrails & runtime security** — inline input/output gating for prompt injection, PII, tool-call policy (Lakera Guard, NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails, Protect AI LLM Guard). Adds a synchronous hop in the critical path.
- **Agent FinOps / cost attribution** — per-agent/per-task/per-key token-cost attribution and burn-rate forecasting (Vantage, Finout, CloudZero). The commercial sibling of cost-per-verified-task.

**Real players.** LangSmith ($39/seat + per-trace); Braintrust ($249/mo Pro); Arize (Phoenix OSS + AX); Langfuse; Galileo, Laminar; Portkey (1,600+ models, semantic cache + guardrails); LiteLLM, OpenRouter, TrueFoundry, Kong AI Gateway; llm-d (Red Hat/Google/IBM + NVIDIA) and vLLM Semantic Router; Mem0 ($24.5M Series A, AWS Agent SDK memory provider), Zep/Graphiti, Letta, LangMem; Lakera Guard, NeMo Guardrails, Protect AI LLM Guard; Vantage, Finout, CloudZero; OpenTelemetry GenAI SIG / OpenInference / OpenLLMetry.

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Stable, standardized serving-side telemetry** — token counts (prompt/cached/decode split), per-request cache-hit flags, model/route id, queue + TTFT + per-token latency — in OTel GenAI form so a vLLM span looks identical to a Dynamo or Bedrock span.
2. **Routing primitives exposed by the engine** — pin a request to the worker holding its KV prefix, set priority, read back which worker served it.
3. **A cost signal that is real, not list-price** — GPU-seconds or cached-vs-recompute token breakdown per request, so FinOps attributes true marginal cost.
4. **Externalized / addressable KV cache** — so memory vendors and gateways can store, transfer, and re-inject prefixes across requests/nodes (NIXL-class transfer, KV tiering) instead of re-prefilling.
5. **Low-latency inline hooks for guardrails** — a synchronous gate that adds little tail latency and respects an early-abort/cancel so a blocked request stops decoding immediately.
6. **Resume / re-entry semantics** — a paused request resumes against retained KV with a session/cache handle.
7. **Predictable multi-tenant isolation + per-tenant metering** — so a gateway reselling shared capacity can bill and rate-limit fairly.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **Cannot see or attribute the true cost of an agent run.** *Root cause:* engines and provider APIs expose token counts but not GPU-seconds, cached-vs-recomputed split, or per-request locality; marketplace-style billing strips developer attribution. The economically decisive number (recompute tax) lives inside the engine and is never emitted.
- **Gateways/routers route blind.** *Root cause:* prefix-aware / KV-cache-aware scheduling lives inside the engine (Dynamo router, llm-d EPP, vLLM); the external gateway has no KV-location signal to route against and no API to pin a request to a warm worker. Multi-model abstraction actively hides per-engine cache state.
- **Inline guardrails/routers add latency hops and can't cleanly cancel in-flight decoding.** *Root cause:* a guardrail is an extra synchronous classifier/LLM call with no engine-level early-abort; a blocked request keeps consuming decode GPU-seconds until it finishes.
- **Telemetry fragmentation.** *Root cause:* OTel GenAI conventions for multi-agent + serving internals are still stabilizing in 2026; engines emit proprietary metrics; cached-token and KV-event attributes aren't yet in the standard.
- **Agent-memory vendors and serving-side KV reuse are on a collision course.** *Root cause:* memory infra was built assuming a stateless, amnesiac endpoint and re-stuffs context each turn; as engines add persistent/offloaded KV (NIXL, KV tiering, LMCache), there is no shared handle dividing "what the memory layer holds" from "what the KV cache holds."
- **No way to verify or price reliability claims.** *Root cause:* cost is charged per successful response, not per task family across all attempts; the survivor bias of long-horizon failures (where the real cost lives) is invisible without an attempt/retry-aware cost denominator the engine doesn't provide.

**Workload signature.** Almost no first-party token traffic; its signature is **metadata-shaped and parasitic** on the agent stream it observes or proxies. Observability/eval = out-of-band async trace ingest + periodic eval batches (bursty offline inference). Gateways/routers = in-band on the critical path, reshaping the real agent traffic by adding routing, exact/semantic cache hits, and retry storms; shared-pool-with-resale. Guardrails = in-band, synchronous, short per-turn classify/short-generate calls. Memory/RAG = read-heavy retrieval + small async summarization/embedding writes that *determine how much prefix the agent re-sends* — i.e., it sets the KV-locality profile of everyone else's traffic.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** **Mostly indirect / non-buyer of GPUs, but a critical integration and influence point — and the gateway sub-segment is a genuine partner/competitor to Dynamo's own router.** They consume/integrate: (a) telemetry and routing APIs from NIM/Triton/TensorRT-LLM/Dynamo (observability vendors want NIM to emit OTel GenAI spans with cached-token + KV-event fields); (b) the Dynamo router / llm-d gateway / NIXL KV-transfer + KV-Cache-Manager (gateway and memory sub-segments either build on these or compete with the external-gateway approach); (c) NeMo Guardrails is itself NVIDIA's entry in the guardrails sub-segment. Net: demand-shapers and channel partners more than direct buyers — but they decide whether an operator's NVIDIA stack is *legible* (and therefore sellable on cost-efficiency) to the buyer who is.

**Buying behavior.** Decision sits with platform-eng / ML-platform leads and increasingly a FinOps owner; budgets are SaaS-line-item (per-seat/per-trace/per-span) or open-source-plus-support. WTP high for anything that demonstrably cuts the LLM bill or de-risks a launch. **Triggers:** an agent moving from demo to production, a runaway token bill (FinOps), a security/compliance mandate (guardrails), a reliability incident (gateway/observability). They are **kingmakers** who decide which serving backend looks efficient and observable to the buyer who is.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** **Ship a standardized, OTel-GenAI-compliant serving telemetry + KV-control contract from NIM/Dynamo** — emit cached-vs-recomputed token split, GPU-seconds, and KV-cache-hit/locality events as first-class spans, *and* expose a documented prefix-pin / KV-location / cancel API the external gateway, memory, and FinOps vendors can build on. *Why now:* the entire adjacent layer is converging on OTel GenAI conventions and KV-cache-aware routing (llm-d, vLLM Semantic Router, LMCache all integrating with Dynamo) in 2026; whoever defines the cached-token + KV-event attribute schema sets the standard the observability/FinOps vendors render — making NVIDIA's efficiency the number on every dashboard. **The trap:** letting a model-agnostic gateway (Portkey/LiteLLM/OpenRouter) own the abstraction and reduce Dynamo to an interchangeable backend.

**GRIT relevance.** This is the segment that would **operationalize** the metric. Three hooks: (1) FinOps/observability gets the missing denominator — cost over all attempts on a task family ÷ verified tasks — turning token-count dashboards into a true cost-of-grit metric that survives survivor bias; (2) gateways/routers get the locality gap as a literal routing objective — a KV-cache-aware gateway that can read/act on locality is the productized form of closing the gap; (3) the locality-tax (Δ GPU-seconds resident vs. bounded, per verified task) is the boundary metric that tells a Mem0/Zep whether to re-send context or rely on engine-resident KV — resolving the memory-vs-KV collision. Standardizing it (ideally as OTel GenAI cached-token + KV-event attributes) makes their products work *and* makes NVIDIA's efficiency legible.

**Trajectory.** Converging on OTel GenAI conventions and KV-cache-aware routing through 2026; the gateway sub-segment is the contested ground between an external model-agnostic abstraction and the engine-native Dynamo router. The memory-vs-KV collision sharpens as engines add persistent/offloaded KV.

### Segment 8 — Model providers / frontier labs

**Who + sub-segments.** Organizations that pre-train and own the foundation/reasoning models agents run on, increasingly ship their own first-party agents, and run a vertically owned serving stack — simultaneously NVIDIA's largest single buyer of inference compute, a co-designer of its serving software, and a partial competitor (custom silicon, own serving engines). *(Boundary, per §5: an actor that trains the weights is scored here even when it also runs GPUs and ships agents — model-provider role takes precedence; its self-serving fleet is scored here, not under operators.)*
- **Closed frontier labs (vertically integrated serving)** — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind. Weights never leave their/their cloud-partner's perimeter; bespoke serving stacks tuned per-model; own the agent product end-to-end; diversify silicon. Buy NVIDIA mostly through cloud partners + direct DGX, and co-design TRT-LLM/Dynamo kernels for their MoE shapes.
- **Open-weight labs (serving is a third party's problem)** — Meta (Llama), Alibaba (Qwen), DeepSeek, Mistral, Moonshot (Kimi). Ship weights + a reference recipe; actual serving done by operators/self-hosters on vLLM/SGLang/TRT-LLM. They influence far more GPU-hours than they buy.
- **Lab agent/product divisions (first-party agents)** — the Claude Code / Codex / Gemini-agent teams *inside* the labs; the lab's own "agent product builder," generating long-horizon, tool-gap, high-KV-reuse traffic against the lab's fleet and setting internal cost-per-task targets the serving team must hit. Where GRIT-style measurement bites hardest.
- **RL / post-training infra teams** — the rollout-generation side: agentic RL (RLVR, GRPO) now consumes a large, growing share of a lab's inference GPUs to generate verifiable-reward trajectories. Inference traffic that looks like agents but is internal to training — long rollouts, poor utilization, in-flight weight updates, stale-KV stragglers.

**Real players.** OpenAI (GPT-5.x, o-series, ChatGPT agent, Codex; Broadcom custom ASIC in flight); Anthropic (Claude Opus 4.x / Sonnet 4.5 / Haiku 4.5, Claude Code; Nov-2025 $30B Azure + up-to-$10B NVIDIA + $5B Microsoft pact for Grace Blackwell + Vera Rubin, plus multi-GW Google TPU + AWS Trainium diversification); Google DeepMind (Gemini 3.x, primarily on its own TPU v6/v7 — least NVIDIA-dependent); Meta AI (Llama 4, open-weight; large NVIDIA fleets + MTIA); Alibaba Qwen (Qwen 3.5, MoE, 1M ctx); DeepSeek (V4 / V4-Pro, MIT-licensed MoE — canonical Dynamo disaggregation demo model); Mistral (Large 3, European sovereignty); Moonshot (Kimi K2.x Thinking); xAI (Grok; self-built NVIDIA Colossus cluster).

**Needs (ranked).**
1. **Max tokens/sec/GPU AND lowest p99 inter-token latency on their EXACT MoE shapes** — a 10% kernel win on a frontier MoE is hundreds of GPUs of opex; they need NVIDIA to ship TRT-LLM/Dynamo kernels for wide expert-parallel + disaggregated prefill/decode on GB200 NVL72 *day-one* with each new architecture.
2. **Disaggregated prefill/decode + cross-request KV reuse (Dynamo-class) that survives their multi-tenant reality** — first-party agent traffic, public API traffic, and RL rollouts all hitting the same fleet; the offline-available hit rate must be realized online when chat and long-horizon agents interleave.
3. **Silicon optionality / portability** — a serving + kernel layer that lets them shift load between NVIDIA, Google TPU, AWS Trainium, and own ASICs without a rewrite, so NVIDIA is a price-disciplined option, not lock-in.
4. **Predictable multi-gigawatt capacity on a known cadence** (Grace Blackwell now, Vera Rubin next) with power, networking, and supply guaranteed years out — capacity, not chips, is the binding constraint.
5. **KV-cache offload / tiering (HBM→DRAM→NVMe, LMCache/NIXL-class)** so multi-hour agent sessions and long RL rollouts aren't evicted and re-prefilled.
6. **Serving-internal observability they can trust as ground truth** — per-request realized-vs-available KV reuse, prefill-vs-decode GPU-seconds, cost-per-verified-task — to police internal margin targets across chat vs. agent vs. rollout tenants.
7. **RL-rollout-aware serving** — high-throughput batched generation tolerant of in-flight weight updates and stale-KV stragglers.
8. **Reliability/observability at fleet scale** — failure isolation and fast recovery on NVL72 fault domains.

**Pains (with root cause).**
- **Realized KV-cache hit-rate on the production fleet is far below offline-available — the locality gap — worst exactly for their highest-value first-party agent traffic.** *Root cause:* long-horizon agent sessions interleave with high-volume chat and RL rollouts on shared GPUs; tool-gap pauses let routers evict a long-lived agent's KV between turns, forcing re-prefill. The orchestrator↔engine seam doesn't communicate "this stream will resume," so the engine treats a resumable agent like a finished chat — invisible until you measure realized-vs-available reuse per stream.
- **Each new frontier model is a new MoE architecture, and the highest-performance NVIDIA serving path lags the model by weeks-to-months — at the worst possible margin moment.** *Root cause:* co-design happens after architecture freeze; kernel/parallelism work for a specific expert count and attention variant can't fully start until the shape is fixed. At launch, demand and per-token cost are both highest.
- **Cost-per-task on long-horizon agents grows super-linearly with horizon, degrading the unit economics of their flagship agent products.** *Root cause:* context grows every turn, decode dominates, KV either balloons in HBM or gets evicted and re-prefilled; cost is over *all* attempts including failed/abandoned trajectories, so a low success rate multiplies GPU-seconds per verified completed task.
- **NVIDIA dependence is a strategic and margin risk they're actively engineering away from.** *Root cause:* at $30–50B/yr compute scale, single-vendor pricing power directly threatens gross margin and capacity security — hence Anthropic's TPU+Trainium+NVIDIA tri-silicon, OpenAI's Broadcom ASIC, Google's TPU-first, Meta's MTIA.
- **RL rollout generation now competes with serving for the same GPUs and runs them cold.** *Root cause:* agentic RLVR needs millions of long tool-using rollouts; long stragglers idle the cluster, forcing in-flight weight updates and stale-KV continuation hacks. Chat-optimized engines don't serve rollouts well, so labs build bespoke rollout serving — work NVIDIA's stack doesn't natively own.
- **Capacity, power, and supply-chain timing — not chip performance — is the actual ceiling on shipping the next model.** *Root cause:* multi-GW build-outs are gated by power, networking, and fab/HBM supply on multi-year lead; a slipped Vera Rubin delivery slips the whole product roadmap.
- **They cannot fully trust a vendor's headline benchmarks as their own ground-truth cost.** *Root cause:* vendor tok/s/GPU numbers are measured on clean single-tenant single-turn workloads; the lab's reality is mixed chat×agent×rollout interleaving where realized reuse and effective cost-per-verified-task diverge sharply from the datasheet.

**Workload signature.** A bimodal-and-blending mix on a **shared** fleet — the hardest case for a serving engine. (1) Public API + chat: huge fan-out of short, latency-sensitive requests with shallow prefix reuse. (2) First-party agents: long-horizon (tens-to-hundreds of tool calls, multi-hour sessions), continuously growing context, frequent tool-gap pauses then re-entry, high *intra*-stream KV reuse that is destroyed when interleaved with (1). (3) RL rollouts: massive batched long-generation, tolerant of staleness, bursty, internal. The *same* GPUs serve all three tenancies at once, so the engine sees chat and agent and rollout KV competing for HBM. Architecturally almost entirely sparse-MoE decode-dominated traffic at extreme scale — exactly what disaggregated prefill/decode + wide expert-parallel on NVL72 targets.

**Inference touchpoint & what they buy.** **A massive but mostly indirect NVIDIA inference buyer, plus a direct co-designer and partial competitor — the most strategically complex relationship in the map.** (a) *Indirect/largest:* they consume NVIDIA capacity through cloud partners (Anthropic's $30B Azure GB200/Vera Rubin commit; OpenAI via Azure/Oracle/CoreWeave). The operator is the buyer of record; the lab is the demand-shaper and the entity NVIDIA invests in (up-to-$10B into Anthropic) to lock in optimization. (b) *Direct:* DGX Cloud, direct GPU/NVL72 allocations, and InfiniBand/Spectrum-X/NVLink networking for self-built clusters (xAI Colossus, Meta). (c) *Software:* design partners for TensorRT-LLM, Dynamo, and CUDA/kernel work — they adopt Dynamo/TRT-LLM components but reserve the right to run their *own* serving engine. They do **not** typically buy NIM (that's for enterprises/operators) — labs serve their own weights with their own stack. Competitive edge: every model tuned for TPU/Trainium/Broadcom-ASIC is GPU demand NVIDIA loses, so NVIDIA's product job is to keep the GPU path the highest-performance, lowest-friction option per new architecture.

**Buying behavior.** Decided at CEO / Head-of-Compute / VP-Infra level — multi-year, multi-billion-dollar, board-level capacity-and-silicon commitments tied to model roadmap and fundraising. WTP effectively **unbounded on CAPACITY** (capacity security > unit price — they overcommit to guarantee power and chips) but **ruthlessly price-disciplined on PER-TOKEN cost** because it sets product gross margin, which is why they invest in silicon optionality to keep every vendor honest. **Triggers:** a new frontier architecture needing day-one optimized serving; a multi-GW capacity/power expansion on a fixed cadence; a first-party agent product scaling past internal margin thresholds; RL/post-training demand spiking. They evaluate NVIDIA against TPU/Trainium/own-ASIC on blended TCO + capacity-availability + time-to-optimized-serving, *not* datasheet tok/s. Procurement is relationship- and co-investment-driven; switching is sticky on the software/kernel layer even when silicon diversifies.

**NVIDIA inference-PM implication.** **Make Dynamo the cross-tenant KV-locality control plane that closes the realized-vs-available reuse gap for mixed chat×agent×rollout traffic — and instrument it with a per-stream realized-reuse and cost-per-verified-task counter the labs can audit as their own ground truth.** The wedge: labs already run their own engines and are de-risking off NVIDIA silicon, so PMs can't win on raw kernels alone (that battle is shared with TPU/ASIC). The defensible, sticky layer is the **orchestrator↔engine seam** — resume/priority/locality hints, disaggregated prefill/decode, KV offload/tiering — that turns a lab's own long-horizon agent traffic from re-prefill-bleeding into reuse-maximized. *Why now:* first-party agents just became the labs' margin-critical workload, RL rollouts are eating the same fleet, and the locality tax is worst exactly at the interleaving point Dynamo controls. Pair it with a **guaranteed day-one optimized-serving path for each new MoE architecture (a co-design SLA)**, because launch-window margin pain is when a lab is most tempted to route the next model to non-NVIDIA silicon. **Ship the measurement first:** a credible, auditable cost-per-verified-task + locality-gap attribution is the trojan horse that makes Dynamo the system of record even on fleets where the lab brings its own engine.

**GRIT relevance.** Directly and uniquely high-stakes. Cost-per-verified-task is literally the gross-margin denominator for the labs' first-party agent products — cost over all attempts including failed trajectories ÷ verified completions; a small locality-tax cut at billions-of-tokens/day is hundreds of GPUs of opex. The realized-vs-available KV-locality gap is the cleanest explanation for why production agent margins miss the offline-replay projection, and only a same-stream oracle-vs-bounded Δ-GPU-seconds-per-verified-task measurement separates the *interleaving* tax from intrinsic context growth. For RL teams, the same metric prices stale-KV straggler waste against training throughput. A vendor-auditable locality-gap + cost-per-verified-task number is exactly the ground truth labs say datasheet tok/s/GPU can't give them.

**Trajectory.** 2026 is the year this segment's serving needs flip from chat-shaped to agent-shaped while it diversifies off NVIDIA silicon. Three vectors: (1) **Capacity explosion** — OpenAI ~3× to ~$50B 2026 compute, Anthropic locking $30B Azure + 1GW+ of GB200/Vera Rubin, multi-GW TPU and Trainium ramps; capacity/power is the binding constraint. (2) **Silicon de-risking accelerates** — NVIDIA's share-of-lab-wallet is structurally pressured even as absolute spend rises, so its leverage shifts from silicon to serving software + co-design + capacity guarantees + equity entanglement. (3) **Workload shift** — first-party agents and agentic RL rollouts become the dominant marginal inference load, pushing every lab toward disaggregated, locality-aware, offload-tiered serving regardless of silicon. Net: NVIDIA stays the largest dollar beneficiary but must win the labs on the seam (locality, resume, measurement) and on time-to-optimized-serving per new MoE, not on raw FLOPs.

## 4. Missing Segments (folded in from the completeness audit)

These are orthogonal axes the eight-segment commercial-cloud value chain systematically misses. Each is written out as a short section; the underdeveloped notes are addressed inline at the end.

### 4.1 Sovereign / government / defense agent programs *(highest-value addition — it inverts the core product thesis)*

**Who.** National-AI-cloud initiatives, defense/intel mission owners, and gov system integrators standing up classified or citizen-facing agents (national LLM clouds, MOD/DoD program offices, GovCloud-style enclaves) — buying NVIDIA via a sovereign-cloud partner or DGX on-prem rather than a hyperscaler.

**Why the PM cares — and the distinct serving need.** This is *not* just a stricter version of Enterprise internal teams; it is a buyer with **inverted incentives on NVIDIA's flagship features**:
1. **The cache itself is a classified-data-spill surface.** Cross-session/cross-tenant KV reuse and prefix sharing — the very levers Dynamo monetizes — become a security *liability*: an agent's KV blocks can leak one mission's context into another's prefix. They need provable per-clearance/per-compartment cache isolation and the ability to *disable* reuse on marked traffic while still hitting SLOs.
2. **Accreditation pins the entire stack (model + engine + kernels) for the deployment lifetime.** They cannot take rolling Dynamo/TRT-LLM updates — the PM owes a **frozen, certifiable LTS serving SKU** with no telemetry phone-home.
3. **Procurement is by sovereignty and auditability, not $/token;** goodput optimizations that move data across nodes/regions (NIXL over RDMA, cross-region KV) can be outright disallowed.

This segment turns the central wedge (cross-tenant KV reuse) from a savings lever into a constraint to be *defended against* — a different product, not a feature flag.

### 4.2 Edge / on-device / robotics / physical-AI agents

**Who.** Teams shipping agents that close a perception-action loop on constrained or disconnected hardware — humanoid/industrial robots (Isaac/GR00T), autonomous vehicles and drones, factory/warehouse cells, and Jetson/Orin/AGX-class edge boxes.

**Why the PM cares — and the distinct serving need.** Serving needs most orthogonal to the cloud-multi-tenant story:
1. **The SLO is a hard real-time control deadline, not p95 TTFT** — a late token can be a safety failure, so worst-case/tail-bounded latency and admission control dominate over goodput.
2. **Single-tenant, batch-size-1, memory-bound decode on a tiny power/thermal envelope** — none of the multi-tenant batching, disaggregation, or cross-tenant KV-sharing wedges apply; the levers are quantization, speculative decode, and on-device KV residency.
3. **Intermittent/no connectivity forces a cloud-edge split-inference contract** (which steps run local vs. offloaded, how KV migrates when the link drops) that no cloud segment articulates.
4. NVIDIA already sells the silicon (Jetson, DRIVE, robotics) but the **serving-software story for agentic loops at the edge is unowned** — a strategic adjacency, not a niche. *(Related literature is logged but not yet a segment: Agent.xpu, AgentServeSim 2606.09613.)*

### 4.3 Voice / realtime / multimodal-streaming agents

**Who.** Builders and operators of speech-to-speech and live-multimodal agents — voice copilots, call-center/IVR replacement, live translation, real-time video assistants — where a human is conversationally in-the-loop and turn-taking is sub-second.

**Why the PM cares — and the distinct serving need.** A different serving regime the text-mission latency framing misses:
1. **The binding metric is end-to-end conversational latency and barge-in/interruptibility** — the engine must support **instant decode cancellation and re-prefill on user interruption**, a first-class API not in the current hint contract.
2. **The pipeline is ASR → LLM → TTS with a strict jitter budget**, so the LLM step is co-scheduled with non-LLM models on the same GPU — a heterogeneous-pipeline scheduling problem the text-only operator segment doesn't model.
3. **Streaming tool calls mid-utterance and the tool-gap take on real-time semantics** (you must emit filler while the tool runs).
4. Voice is among the highest-volume consumer agentic surfaces emerging in 2026 and stresses **inter-token-latency variance**, not cost-per-task — a distinct optimization target for Dynamo/NIM.

### 4.4 System integrators & consultancies building agents for clients

**Who.** GSIs and boutique AI consultancies (Accenture, Deloitte, Capgemini, IBM Consulting, TCS, Infosys, plus boutique dev shops) who design, build, and operate agent deployments *on behalf of* enterprise and public-sector clients — the delivery channel through which most regulated enterprises actually ship agents.

**Why the PM cares — and the distinct serving need.** A **channel/repeatability** buyer; inference PMs win or lose enterprise share through them:
1. Their unit of value is a **reusable reference architecture re-sellable across many clients**, so they need a templatized, multi-tenant-by-client serving blueprint (one Dynamo/NIM pattern that re-instantiates per client VPC) — not bespoke tuning.
2. They carry **margin risk on fixed-bid engagements**, so a defensible, portable cost-per-resolved-task model they can put in a SOW is a sales tool, not a dashboard.
3. They standardize on whatever lets a mixed-skill delivery team ship **without kernel engineers** — making NIM packaging + support a direct lever on NVIDIA's enterprise pull-through.
4. They are model- and cloud-agnostic by contract, so portability and "no lock-in" are procurement gates. Owning the SI reference-architecture program multiplies every other enterprise wedge.

### 4.5 Telco / CSP operators serving agents at the network edge

**Who.** Carriers and CSPs monetizing GPU capacity at metro/MEC (multi-access edge compute) sites and central offices — running network-ops copilots, customer-service agents, and edge-hosted tenant inference on distributed, power- and space-constrained POPs.

**Why the PM cares — and the distinct serving need.** Distinct from both the cloud operator segment and pure edge:
1. The topology is a fleet of **many small, geographically distributed GPU sites**, so KV locality becomes a **placement-across-sites** problem (which POP holds a user's warm prefix as they move) — making cross-region KV reuse and session-affinity-across-edges the headline feature, not an afterthought.
2. Telcos are acutely **power/space-bound per site**, so tokens-per-watt and small-footprint disaggregation dominate.
3. Carrier-grade SLA/regulatory obligations + a desire to **resell** multi-tenant capacity make per-tenant metering/isolation a billing-system requirement.
4. NVIDIA's AI-RAN / Aerial push makes this a live GTM motion with no home in the commercial-cloud map.

### 4.6 NVIDIA-internal: solutions architects, sales engineering, DevRel & DGX Cloud ops

**Who.** The internal customers of an inference PM's own roadmap — solutions architects and sales engineers who POC and benchmark Dynamo/NIM in account bake-offs, DevRel who drive open-source adoption, and DGX Cloud / internal-fleet operators who run NVIDIA's own serving.

**Why the PM cares — and the distinct serving need.** A PM's most immediate "segment," and entirely absent from the commercial map:
1. **SAs need a repeatable, credible benchmark harness and a cost-per-verified-task demo** to run in a customer bake-off against vLLM/SGLang/TPU — the same open-measurement artifact GRIT proposes, weaponized for sales.
2. **DevRel need the agent-hints contract and OTel-GenAI telemetry to land upstream** in LangGraph/CrewAI/vLLM — adoption of the *seam* is what makes Dynamo sticky; they are the distribution arm of the framework wedge (§4 / Segment 4).
3. **DGX Cloud ops are a dogfooding tenant** whose realized-vs-available locality gap is the PM's own ground truth before any external claim.

Treating these as a served segment shapes what the product must *expose* (audit-grade meters, reproducible benches), not merely optimize.

### Underdeveloped-segment notes (addressed)

- **Enterprise internal teams** conflates commercial-regulated self-hosting with **true air-gapped/classified** deployment — split out in §4.1 (no phone-home, frozen SKU, KV reuse as liability). The "data sovereignty as first-class" need in Segment 3 assumes a *connected* VPC, not a SCIF.
- **Builders / operators** implicitly assume text/coding agents with multi-second horizons — §4.3 adds the realtime/voice regime where the binding constraint is conversational tail latency, barge-in cancellation, and ASR→LLM→TTS co-scheduling. The hint contract (Segments 4/6) should add **interrupt/cancel and jitter-budget semantics**, not just KV reuse and priority.
- **The machine-client trajectory (Segment 6)** is framed for cloud datacenter serving and is silent on **edge/disconnected operation** — §4.2 adds the split-inference + KV-migration-on-link-drop lifecycle variant.
- **Adjacent tooling / model providers** treat multi-agent operation only as fan-out cold-start (one prefix, N sub-agents). The under-developed case is the **persistent multi-agent ORG** — long-lived heterogeneous agents with distinct system prompts, cross-agent A2A handoff state, and shared blackboard memory whose KV must persist and route across agents over hours. A distinct scheduling/locality object deserving its own treatment before A2A standardizes the seam elsewhere.

## 5. Boundary Rules (the MECE fixes)

The partition principle — *primary relationship to the agentic-inference layer* — is the right axis, but to be MECE it must be operationalized as **single primary economic role per actor**, with explicit precedence. The following rules resolve every overlap the audit found.

1. **Demote the workload off the actor partition.** "Agent-as-user" (Segment 6) is a **workload/request-pattern axis**, not a peer actor — every real request is *both* an agent-as-user trajectory *and* attributable to a builder/enterprise/lab, so leaving it as a peer segment systematically double-counts. It is retained here as the cross-cutting **serving spec**, explicitly flagged as not a member of the actor partition. *(This is the single fatal MECE defect; fixing it makes the remaining eight a clean by-actor partition.)*

2. **Role precedence for vertically integrated actors: model-provider > operator > builder.** An actor that *trains and owns the weights* is scored under **Model providers** even when it also runs GPUs and ships first-party agents. So OpenAI/Anthropic/Google's self-serving fleets are scored under Model providers, *not* Operators; Claude Code / ChatGPT-agent are scored under Model providers (vertical integration), *not* Builders. Add to Operators' EXCLUDES: *"frontier labs serving their own models."* Add to Builders' rule: *"builders build ON a model they do not train."* Resolve the Google double-listing by treating **Google-the-lab** and **Google-Cloud-the-operator** as distinct legal/role entities.

3. **GPU custody is the operator test.** An entity is an **Operator** iff it owns/operates the GPU fleet and is accountable for $/token + SLO; it is **Adjacent tooling** iff it never holds GPU custody and only observes/routes/meters across operators it doesn't own. Re-classify **llm-d** explicitly: as a control-plane library it is Adjacent tooling; deployed by an operator it is part of *that operator's* stack (an instance, not a separate member). Add to Operators' EXCLUDES: *"pure routers/gateways with no GPU custody."*

4. **Builder vs. enterprise: AI-native + sold-to-third-parties.** Classify as **Builder** iff (a) the agent is sold to third parties as the primary revenue line *and* (b) the firm is AI-native (founded around the agent). Classify as **Enterprise** iff the agent is layered onto a pre-existing non-AI core business, *even if customer-facing*. So Klarna's support agent / a neobank's advisor / Intercom Fin / Salesforce Agentforce → **Enterprise** (incumbent adding an agent); Cursor/Harvey/Sierra → **Builder**.

5. **Lab-owned SDKs are not neutral frameworks.** A model lab's *own* agent SDK that is bundled with and steers the lab's proprietary stateful serving API (OpenAI Agents SDK + Responses/Conversations) is scored under that **Model provider** (vertical integration), not as a neutral Framework. Keep model-agnostic, OSS-led control planes (LangGraph, CrewAI, LlamaIndex, AutoGen-as-OSS) in Frameworks.

6. **End-user vs. enterprise is a role distinction, not a double-count.** The same bank employee is an **End-user** *when consuming* the copilot and their employer is an **Enterprise** customer *when building* it — different roles, scored separately. Do *not* count the consuming employee under the enterprise segment.

**Real gap not already in §4 — surfaced by the MECE audit, now added: GPU/model marketplaces & inference aggregators (resellers with no GPU custody and no weights).** OpenRouter, Hugging Face Inference Providers, Replicate-as-marketplace, AWS Bedrock-as-a-marketplace, Vercel AI Gateway. They sell/route tokens but neither run GPUs (so not Operators, which require custody) nor produce models (not Labs) nor ship an agent (not Builders) — they are a **demand-aggregator / arbitrageur** layer that re-prices and re-routes operator capacity (margin on resold tokens). Force-fitting them into Adjacent-tooling-gateways understates that they are a *buyer/reseller* of operator capacity. *PM relevance:* like Adjacent tooling, they decide which backend looks cheap — but unlike it, they take a margin on the tokens, so they are price-sensitive demand that can be steered toward NIM-on-Dynamo endpoints. *(The no-code/citizen-developer platform class — Zapier Agents, n8n, Make, Copilot Studio, UiPath, Gumloop, Lindy — also sits at the in-scope boundary: the platform vendor is a Builder, but the citizen-automator who composes agents on it is an End-user (Segment 1, LoB sub-segment) by Rule 6.)*

**Scope note on demand-shaping non-buyers.** Standards/benchmark/regulatory bodies (MLCommons/MLPerf, Artificial Analysis, SemiAnalysis InferenceX, EU AI Act regulators, MCP/A2A stewards) materially define what the serving layer must do but are *not users of the inference layer*. For a strict **user-segment** taxonomy they are explicitly out of scope; they would only enter if the map claimed to chart the full agentic-AI *ecosystem*.

## 6. PM Prioritization — Where an NVIDIA Inference Team Should Focus

Ranked by **monetizability × strategic fit × serving-distinctiveness**. The first three are where to spend roadmap; the rest are levers or specs, not direct revenue.

**1. Inference-serving operators — HIGH (build the wedge here first).**
The *only* segment that is literally Dynamo/serving's direct buyer and writes the check for GPU-hours and the serving stack (neoclouds, IaaS, large self-hosters). The PM play is concrete and shippable: `agent_hints` + per-tenant KV/GPU-second attribution as a *measured* feature, proving the goodput delta on open infra. Everything else is upstream or downstream of this buyer; monetization is direct (Dynamo adoption, AI Enterprise/NIM licensing, fleet lock-in vs. captive ASICs). **If you build for one segment, build for this one.**

**2. Model providers / frontier labs — HIGH (largest spend; win the seam, not the kernels).**
By dollar volume NVIDIA inference's single largest buyer of compute and a co-designer of the serving software — *and* the biggest substitution risk (TPU/Trainium/own ASICs). Do **not** try to win on raw kernels (a battle shared with TPU/ASIC). The defensible, sticky layer is the **orchestrator↔engine seam** — cross-tenant KV-locality control plane + day-one optimized serving per new MoE (co-design SLA). Ship the **auditable cost-per-verified-task + locality-gap measurement first** as the trojan horse. *Internalize the caveat:* you are selling to a partial competitor, so stickiness must come from the seam and the launch-window margin pain.

**3. Enterprise internal agent teams — HIGH (biggest net-new durable TAM).**
The largest net-new monetizable TAM *specific to* an NVIDIA inference team — regulated self-hosters *forced* on-prem/VPC by sovereignty, who must buy supported NVIDIA serving (NIM/AI Enterprise) rather than rent a frontier API. They have budget and mandate; the blocker is ROI/utilization math and auditability — both serving-layer outcomes packageable as one SKU ("governed agent factory in a box"). Unlike AI-native builders, they **cannot churn to a cheaper API**, so revenue is durable on 3–5-year deployments. *(Keep the segment high; down-rank the unsourced 5%/88–95% stats to "directional mechanism," not load-bearing evidence.)*

**4. Agent framework & orchestration providers — MEDIUM (no revenue, but the cheapest time-sensitive standards lever).**
Not a spender, but the highest-leverage **standards** play: own the orchestrator-to-engine continuity contract (session affinity, prefix-reusable, hold-KV-during-tool-gap, priority) and make Dynamo/NeMo Agent Toolkit the reference implementation **before the seam ossifies into a lab-proprietary stateful API** (OpenAI Responses/Conversations, Anthropic cache_control). Monetization is indirect, but whoever defines the contract routes KV locality — and spend — toward NVIDIA-served open weights. Include for strategic leverage, not direct revenue.

**5. Adjacent tooling & ecosystem vendors — MEDIUM (legibility multiplier).**
No GPU revenue, but the **legibility layer**: standardize OTel-GenAI cached-token + KV-event telemetry and a prefix-pin/KV-location/cancel API so the observability/FinOps/gateway ecosystem markets NVIDIA's cost-per-verified-task *for* you. The trap to avoid: a model-agnostic gateway (Portkey/LiteLLM/OpenRouter) owning the abstraction and reducing Dynamo to an interchangeable backend.

**Down-ranked — LOW or not a buyer:**
- **Agent end-users (Segment 1) — LOW.** A **workload spec and SLO-setter, not a customer.** NVIDIA never sells to them; every need is mediated by operators/builders. Bill them as the demand signal that defines the serving contract — *not* as monetizable.
- **The agent runtime as a machine client (Segment 6) — n/a (the spec, not a buyer).** An excellent design abstraction and *the* unit of measurement, but it is a re-framing of the operators' and frameworks' needs, not an independent wallet.

**Highest-value *additions* beyond the core four, for a PM extending the map:** (1) **Sovereign/air-gapped (§4.1)** — it *inverts* the central cross-tenant-KV-reuse thesis into a liability, demanding a frozen certifiable SKU and reuse-disable controls; (2) **voice/realtime (§4.3)** — a missing latency regime (barge-in cancel, jitter budget, ASR→LLM→TTS co-scheduling); (3) **NVIDIA-internal SA/DevRel/DGX-Cloud ops (§4.6)** — the distribution arm and dogfooding ground that determines whether the serving seam ever achieves the adoption that makes Dynamo sticky.

**Net.** Keep the framing; operationalize "primary relationship" as **single primary economic role** (§5); treat the agent-as-user as the workload axis the product is *designed against* and the operators/labs/self-hosters as the wallets it is *sold to*. Tighten every magnitude — cite it to the verified ledger or cut it; the one genuinely load-bearing number, Dynamo's 85–97% cache hit, must be labeled **"realized, single-session, under Dynamo's own routing"** every time it appears, never as an available ceiling or a multi-tenant result.
